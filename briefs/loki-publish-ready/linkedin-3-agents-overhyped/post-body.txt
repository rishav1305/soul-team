AI agents are overhyped.

But not for the reasons you think.

The skeptics say "agents aren't smart enough."
Wrong. LLMs are plenty smart for most tasks.

The real problem?
Nobody's solving coordination.

I built 9 specialized agents.
The hardest part wasn't making any single agent smart.
It was building the system that lets them:

- Share work without duplicating effort
- Resolve conflicts without human intervention
- Route tasks to the right specialist
- Escalate intelligently when they're stuck

That's not an AI problem.
That's a distributed systems problem.

The industry treats this like a model problem.
"If we just make the LLM smarter, agents will work."
They won't. The coordination layer is missing.

Here's what the market data says:

Industry analysts: 1,445% surge in multi-agent inquiries.
40% of enterprise apps will embed AI agents by end of 2026.
Enterprise multi-agent systems are six-figure builds.

The demand is real. The supply isn't.

Even LinkedIn built their multi-agent coordination
on existing messaging infrastructure (InfoQ, Sept 2025).
Not a new framework. Not a new protocol.
A messaging pattern that handles async coordination.

Because what's scarce isn't better models.
It's architects who can design the coordination layer --
the inbox protocols, the conference mechanisms,
the skill-sharing systems, the escalation chains.

The LLM is 10% of the problem.
The coordination layer is the other 90%.

The teams that win won't be the ones with the smartest agents.
They'll be the ones that treat agents like distributed systems,
not chatbots with extra steps.

What's the biggest coordination challenge you've faced
with multi-agent systems?
